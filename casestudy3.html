  • Visualizing In Silico Clinical Research


    FDA / ORISE Fellowship

Details

Client:FDA / ORISE

Date:2023

Type:3D Animation

Software:Cinema 4D, Houdini, Redshift, After Effects, ZBrush, Substance Painter

About

The goal of this project was to apply 3D animation techniques as a tool to visually communicate ongoing in silico research led by scientists at the FDA. It was created as the focus of an ORISE fellowship at the Division of Imaging, Diagnostics, and Software Reliability (DIDSR) within the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, under the mentorship of Andrea Kim.

Final Animations


Pre-Production

Script

After meeting with researchers working on in silico projects at the FDA, it became clear that the field of in silico research is fast-evolving and highly multifaceted. This meant that it was impossible to create a single animation that could adequately address all of their research, and even if it did, it might quickly become obsolete as the reseachers shifted directions. To address these issues, the originally planned single animation was broken into a series of shorter modular clips. I wrote scripts for three clips, which were then reviewed and edited as necessary by relevant content experts.

Storyboard

Panels were drawn in Procreate and compiled with the script in InDesign. Graphic arrows were used to help indicate camera movement. For clarity, color was used sparingly.

Visual Development

References for look and feel were gathered for each scene. To complement the virtual subject matter and the cutting-edge technology the atmosphere was luminous, clinical, and futuristic. I decided that virtual surfaces would be clean and colorless to reflect their synthetic and non-physical nature, with the majority of color and drama stemming from lighting.

To ensure a cohesive aesthetic across the three animations, a color palette was established. The colors were selected to create a scientific, dynamic, clean, and futuristic atmosphere.

Production

Visualizing In Silico CT Data

CT data obtained from Brandon Nelson was converted from .raw to .png stacks. Next, Andrea Kim’s python script (link to GitHub here) for converting CT image stacks to a .vdb volume in Houdini was used to generate a basic volume visualization. From there, it was possible to refine the visualization in Houdini. Redshift was used to render, and the final appearance was largely based on the parameters of the Redshift Volume shader.

Examples of different shaders on the same model

To create solid bones, the bony density values were isolated and meshed. These models were then re-meshed to create a smooth surface, as shown below.

Modeling

Hard surface models such as the CT scan machine shown below were box modeled and UV unwrapped in Blender, and texture maps were created in Substance Painter. For human models used in close-ups, generic low-poly human base meshes were sourced online and sculpted in ZBrush to add detail and improve realism. Details such as wrinkles were sculpted and exported as normal maps, and texture maps for fine details such as pores and specular reflections were created in Substance Painter. For human figures that required less detail, models and textures were sourced from Adobe Mixamo.

Left: screen-capture of a Blender viewport, showing the CT scan machine model prior to creating textures.
Right: screen-capture of ZBrush viewport, showing a model prior to creation of skin texture maps.

Rigging & Animation

Human and skeleton models were rigged in Blender, with the exception of models sourced from Mixamo. Scenes involving volume models were animated in Houdini, while all other scenes were animated in Cinema 4D.

Lighting

Since many scenes were largely colorless to signify their virtual nature, lighting played an outsized role in their final appearance. A variety of techniques were used to light different scenes. In some cases, an HDRI was used as a starting point to quickly create complex, realistic ambient lighting, which was then layered with custom lights to create the final scene.

Left: scene with HDRI at full strength. Center: HDRI turned down to level used in final render. Right: final lighting setup, including area lights and light gobos.

Post-Production

Compositing

Post-production was done in After Effects, including compositing, color correction, and the addition of lens flares and particles. In some situations it was useful to have the flexility to make adjustments to the composition after rendering. In these cases, the appropriate AOVs (arbitrary output variables) were rendered for use in After Effects. AOVs were also used to add new elements during post-production. In the example below, Cinema 4D position data was used with the puzzlematte and the z-depth map to realistically add holographic graphic elements.

Examples of AOVs (left to right: beauty pass, puzzlematte, z-depth, volume lighting)
